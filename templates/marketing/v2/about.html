About

The story you told is not the story that happened.

That's a hundred-year-old observation, not an accusation. The Russian formalists named the gap in 1925: fabula is what happened in the world of the story, syuzhet is the order in which the storyteller chose to reveal it. Every flashback exploits the gap. Every cold open assumes it.

It's a useful distinction, and a hard one to encode. The system most tools build tracks one side — when the audience saw a thing. Fabula also tracks who knew it at that moment, who acted on it, and who carried it forward into the next scene.

Why this matters in plain terms: when a writers' room asks “what does this character believe right now?” and a continuity supervisor asks “what has this character already done by this point?”, they're asking different questions of the same show. Most tools collapse them into one timeline of dialogue. We don't.

§ 01 — every entity, a queryable journey

Every character a traversable arc.

Where most tools collapse a show into one timeline of dialogue, Fabula gives every character (and every location, organisation, and persistent object) its own queryable journey. Every event the entity participates in carries that entity's state at that moment — for a character, that's beliefs, goals, emotional state, and what was observed in the scene.

A spine of psychological state runs down each character, anchored in the Episode → Act → Scene → Beat structure of the show. Cross-episode profiles synthesise role, dilemma, and key relationships per episode and per season; cross-season arcs roll up onto the canonical entity itself. That's how the Brigadier reads as one continuous person across thirteen seasons of Doctor Who.

“What did Bartlet believe in this scene?”
“What had he done by this episode?”
“What changed in him between this season and the next?”
Different operations. All answerable on the same per-entity arc.

§ 02 — we picked shows that would break us

Five series. Five things we knew would break.

Most extraction systems demo on a single film with a small cast. We picked five long-running, structurally awkward shows and ran them through the pipeline knowing each one would expose a different failure. Then we fixed each failure. The table below is the catalogue of those.

ShowWhat it brokeWhat we built to fix it
Doctor Who Identity continuity One canonical Doctor across seven actors and 26 seasons of Classic Who. Regeneration as a tracked transition on the same entity, not a new character.
Star Trek: TNG Alias resolution “Picard,” “The Captain,” “Jean-Luc,” “Locutus.” Promotions, ranks, possessions. Cross-season identity preserved across 177 episodes.
The West Wing Relationship density Dialogue-heavy ensemble where every relationship has a state. Typed edges. Per-event BDI. Mrs. Landingham reverberating across two seasons after her death works at all because of this.
Motherland: Fort Salem Realist ontology Magic that obeys consistent rules requires the graph to track world-internal physics. Soft constraints, hard contradictions.
Indiana Jones Episode boundaries Not a series. Three films, decades apart. One entity graph spans them — the same Indy, the same Marion, the same Sallah, resolved across releases instead of treated as separate productions.

We didn't pick easy material and call the system general. We picked material that would break a brittle system, and built one that didn't break on these shows. “Didn't break” means: ran end-to-end without human intervention, produced a queryable graph, survived a manual continuity audit on a sampled subset of episodes. Things still go wrong on a new show; we expect that, which is why the pipeline checkpoints and recovers instead of trusting first runs.

§ 03 — what we believe

Three convictions you can hold us to.

01.

A story is a web of typed relationships. A spreadsheet is a list of rows. Pretending the second can do the job of the first is what produces story bibles that drift.

02.

Tracking only the order events arrive on screen gets you something that looks correct and quietly isn't. Each character also has to carry their own beliefs, their own goals, and a record of what they've done — separately, queryably, and with provenance.

03.

Hallucination is an engineering problem, not a fundamental one. Schema enforcement, provenance pointers, confidence scoring, and rejection on disagreement solve most of it. We won't claim it's solved — but the shape of the work is engineering, not prayer, and that's what lets a writers' room actually trust the answer.
