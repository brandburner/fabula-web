About

The story you told is not the story that happened.

That's a hundred-year-old observation, not an accusation. The Russian formalists named the gap in 1925: fabula is what happened in the world of the story, syuzhet is the order in which the storyteller chose to reveal it. Every flashback exploits the gap. Every cold open assumes it.

It's a useful distinction, and a hard one to encode. The system most tools build tracks one side — when the audience saw a thing. Fabula also tracks who knew it at that moment, who acted on it, and who carried it forward into the next scene.

§ 01 — every entity, a queryable journey

Every character a traversable arc.

Most tools give one timeline. Fabula gives every character (and every entity) their own, tracking state—beliefs, goals, emotions—at each event.

A spine of psychological state runs down each character, anchored in the Episode → Act → Scene → Beat structure of the show. Cross-episode profiles synthesise role, dilemma, and key relationships per episode and per season; cross-season arcs roll up onto the canonical entity itself. That's how the Brigadier reads as one continuous person across thirteen seasons of Doctor Who, or how Thomas Cromwell carries one private set of loyalties across six episodes of Wolf Hall while wearing a different one in court. (Engineering detail: how we roll episode profiles up into season profiles up into the canonical arc.)

And because identity lives on the canonical entity rather than the show that introduced it, the spine extends past the boundary of any one production. Sarah Jane Smith picks up in The Sarah Jane Adventures as the same agent who left the TARDIS in 1976, with thirty years of belief and circumstance carried forward into a separate series in the same universe. Spin-offs, crossovers, and shared-universe sequels stop being a continuity problem and start being a graph traversal.

“What did Cromwell believe in this scene?”
“What had he done by this episode?”
“What changed in him between this season and the next?”
Different operations. All answerable on the same per-entity arc.

§ 02 — we picked shows that would break us

Four series. Four things we knew would break.

Most extraction systems demo on a single film with a small cast. We picked four long-running, structurally awkward shows and ran them through the pipeline knowing each one would expose a different failure. Then we fixed each failure. The table below is the catalogue of those.

ShowWhat it brokeWhat we built to fix it
Doctor Who Long-running ensemble identity Brigadier Lethbridge-Stewart resolved as one canonical entity across thirteen seasons of Classic Who, despite rank changes, retirements, and multi-year gaps between appearances.
Star Trek: TNG Alias resolution “Picard,” “The Captain,” “Jean-Luc,” “Locutus.” Promotions, ranks, possessions. Cross-season identity preserved across 177 episodes.
The West Wing Relationship density Dialogue-heavy ensemble where every relationship has a state. Typed edges. Per-event BDI. Mrs. Landingham reverberating across two seasons after her death works at all because of this.
Indiana Jones Episode boundaries Not a series. Three films, decades apart. One entity graph spans them — the same Indy, the same Marion, the same Sallah, resolved across releases instead of treated as separate productions.

We chose shows that would break a brittle system, and built one that didn’t. That means it ran end-to-end, passed a manual check, and produced a usable graph — with pipeline checkpoints to handle the unexpected.

§ 03 — what we believe

Three convictions you can hold us to.

01.

A story is a web of typed relationships. A spreadsheet is a list of rows. Pretending the second can do the job of the first is what produces story bibles that drift.

The “web” framing we owe to Paul Rissen, whose work on the BBC's Mythology Engine and his subsequent three-part model for understanding stories sketched the shape of this argument a decade before we picked up the thread.

02.

Tracking only the order events arrive on screen gets you something that looks correct and quietly isn't. Each character also has to carry their own beliefs, their own goals, and a record of what they've done — separately, queryably, and with provenance.

03.

Hallucination is an engineering problem, not a fundamental one. Schema enforcement, provenance pointers, confidence scoring, and rejection on disagreement solve most of it. We won't claim it's solved — but the shape of the work is engineering, not prayer, and that's what lets a continuity supervisor, a VFX bidder, or an LLM actually trust the answer.

§ 04 — contact

Small team. Long memory.

Fabula is a small engineering group building one thing carefully. We came out of production, research, and infrastructure. We've watched too much television; we took good notes.

Most of what's worth reading is already published. Fifty-six datasets are public on brandburner. The catalog at /explore is live. The engine docs are on this site. We also write about the engineering as we build it at storygraph.substack.com — entries including the graph is the story, epistemic isolation, community detection in the TARDIS, and teaching a knowledge graph to learn from “no.” Read what's there first; we'll move faster when you do.
