This might read like trend alignment, but the engineering decisions we made to solve continuity — stable identities, typed connections, per-event belief state, source provenance — happen to be exactly what each of these adjacent markets needs. We didn't pivot to fit them; the same graph fits.

§ 01 — four theses, one company

Four investable categories. One product.

Fabula is intelligible to investors looking at any of these four theses. The same engineering serves all of them; we don't have to pick which one to be.

Thesis 01

Context Graph

Foundation Capital's late-2025 thesis on context graphs argued that the next layer of AI infrastructure is the graph that captures why a decision was made, not just what was decided. Fabula is that graph for the entertainment vertical.

Thesis 02

Vertical Knowledge Graph

Knowledge graphs underpin most large software companies you can name. The Semantic Knowledge Graphing market grows at 14.2% CAGR through 2030. Television production is the largest vertical without a canonical graph; we're the first credible attempt at one.

Thesis 03

World Model

World models are the prediction targets behind the next generation of AI agents. Fabula extracts a narrative-state world model from existing material — belief, goal, and intention transitions per character per scene — which is the input most agents over story material need and don't have. We get there by sidestepping the long-context limitation that bites every team that tries to ask one model to hold a hundred episodes in mind: no single call ever sees more than a few kilobytes.

Thesis 04

Synthetic Training Data

The synthetic data market sat near $600M in 2024 and is forecast past $10B by the mid-2030s on a 30%+ CAGR. The drivers aren't going anywhere: privacy law, the cost of acquiring real data, and the data wall every foundation-model team is now staring at. Fabula makes a particular flavour of synthetic data — structured, sourced, adjudicated against evidence — from material that's hard to scrape and harder still to interpret correctly. We already publish it: the catalogue sits on Hugging Face today.

§ 02 — not the same graph as Netflix's

We operate one layer below the catalogue graphs.

The streaming platforms have invested heavily in catalogue knowledge graphs — titles, talent, genres, ratings. Those graphs power recommendation, discovery, and rights management. They are good at the questions a viewer or a programmer asks.

They cannot answer questions inside a single script. The catalogue knows that The West Wing stars Allison Janney; it does not know that her character has 17 scenes in this draft, gained 3 since the last revision, and triggers two new locations the location department has not yet scouted. That second kind of knowledge is what a line producer, a 1st AD, a VFX coordinator, and a department head all need on the same morning, and it's where Fabula sits.

Catalogue graphs serve consumption. Story graphs serve production. They don't compete; they don't even ask the same questions.

And the engineering shape of the problem — extracting a coherent record from material that's far longer than any single model call can reliably handle — is what makes the architecture transferable. Same pipeline shape, applied to court records, regulatory filings, scientific corpora, archival journalism. Entertainment is where we proved it; it isn't the limit of where it goes.

§ 02a — the data wall, and what we make instead

High-quality text is exhausted. Scraping a second time around makes it worse.

Two findings, both 2024, define the headwind coming for foundation models. Epoch AI's data-scaling analysis projects high-quality language data exhausted between 2026 and 2032 — the well isn't dry, but it's measurable. And in Nature, Shumailov et al. show that models trained recursively on their own output collapse: tails of the distribution disappear, generations degenerate, the original signal is lost. Their canonical demonstration: feed the model text on medieval architecture; by the ninth generation it's outputting a list of jackrabbits.

The implication has flowed downstream pretty quickly. The next decade of training depends on synthetic data that isn't a re-roll of the model's prior output. Structured. Sourced. Adjudicated against evidence. Built deliberately, not scraped from a web that's increasingly the model's own exhaust.

That's the shape of what Fabula produces. Every event, every character, every connection carries a pointer back to the line of script it came from. Belief and intention states aren't free-form generation; they're inferred from dialogue under explicit policy, pinned to a specific scene. The output is a typed graph of narrative facts — structurally distinct from the screenplay it was extracted from, sitting in our schema, generated by our pipeline, organised by our taxonomy. The source was someone else's. The record is ours. We've been publishing portions of it as open datasets on Hugging Face since 2024.

Existing material in. New, structured, queryable data out — data that didn't exist before we made it. That's the asset.
