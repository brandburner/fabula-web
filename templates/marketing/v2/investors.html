§ 01 — three theses, one company

Three investable categories. One product.

Fabula is intelligible to investors looking at any of these three theses. The same engineering serves all three; we don't have to pick which one to be.

We're aware these read like trend alignment. The honest version: the engineering decisions we made to solve continuity — stable identities, typed connections, per-event belief state, source provenance — happen to be exactly what each of these adjacent markets needs. We didn't pivot to fit them; the same graph fits.

Thesis 01

Context Graph

Foundation Capital's late-2025 thesis on context graphs argued that the next layer of AI infrastructure is the graph that captures why a decision was made, not just what was decided. Fabula is that graph for the entertainment vertical.

Thesis 02

Vertical Knowledge Graph

Knowledge graphs underpin most large software companies you can name. The Semantic Knowledge Graphing market grows at 14.2% CAGR through 2030. Television production is the largest vertical without a canonical graph; we're the first credible attempt at one.

Thesis 03

World Model

World models are the prediction targets behind the next generation of AI agents. Fabula extracts a narrative-state world model from existing material — belief, goal, and intention transitions per character per scene — which is the input most agents over story material need and don't have.

§ 02 — not the same graph as Netflix's

We operate one layer below the catalogue graphs.

The streaming platforms have invested heavily in catalogue knowledge graphs — titles, talent, genres, ratings. Those graphs power recommendation, discovery, and rights management. They are good at the questions a viewer or a programmer asks.

They cannot answer questions inside a single show. The catalogue knows that The West Wing stars Allison Janney; it does not know that her character was promoted to Press Secretary in S2E03. That second kind of knowledge is what a writers' room, a continuity supervisor, and a story-aware AI all need, and it's where Fabula sits.

Catalogue graphs serve consumption. Story graphs serve production. They don't compete; they don't even ask the same questions.

§ 04 — what's hard to copy

Three things any competitor would need to replicate.

The pipeline

Ten months of compositional-extraction R&D. 25 node labels, 44 relationship types, model-fallback logic, a per-step prompt library tuned by genre. Most of the difficulty is in the joins between steps; that's where the LLM-only approaches break.

The corpus

Seven series, 800+ episodes, deliberately picked to cover the failure modes (regeneration, alias drift, dense ensemble, magic, deep flashback). The pipeline has been hardened against each of these. A new entrant rebuilding from scratch would have to repeat that breaking-and-fixing work.

The position in the workflow

Built for the writers' room and the continuity-supervision step — that's where the canonical record gets built. Once a show is on Fabula, the canonical record is the Fabula graph. Switching cost compounds with episode count.
