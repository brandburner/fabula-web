§ 01 — three verbs

Parse · Resolve · Reason

These are the three things Fabula does. Pick one to see the actual output.

What that gets you: the script supervisor's question, the AI agent's retrieval, and the writers' room's precedent search are answered against the same record — with the line of script that justifies each answer attached.

Plain text, Fountain, PDF. No reformatting, no template. Fabula reads scenes, beats, dialogue, and unspoken stage business in a single pass.

Saturday Night (2024) doesn't use INT/EXT headings at all — most parsers fail on it. Fabula pulled out 53 scenes, 199 beats, 55 characters on the first run, with no per-show configuration.¹

The script calls Picard "The Captain" in one scene and "Jean-Luc" in the next. Worf gets promoted twice. The Doctor changes face seven times across Classic Who. Fabula keeps each of them as one entity, with the rank changes and regenerations recorded as transitions on that entity rather than as new ones.

When two entities really are different — two characters who happen to share a name, or two ships called Enterprise — Fabula makes the call explicitly and records why, with a confidence score. Adjudication, not vector similarity.

Doctor Who seasons 1–26 covers seven incarnations; the megagraph holds them as one canonical entity, with regenerations recorded as transitions on that entity rather than as new ones.

§ 02 — events have reasons

Most extraction stops at what happened.

Fabula keeps going. For every event in the show it records the connections to other events — typed, not just inferred from proximity. A connection is causal, or it carries a character forward, or it parallels a different event thematically, or it reverses one. Each connection is its own claim, with its own justification.

That's the difference between a transcript and a graph. A transcript can tell you Q showed up and Picard demanded his identity. The graph can tell you the second event happened because of the first, and that the same pattern — institutional authority confronted by individual conviction — recurs four other times across the season.

What that gets you: you can ask “where else have we set up this confrontation, and how did it pay off?” and get four cited scenes back — instead of a writer's room trying to remember.

  strength: 0.74
  description: "Both events stage a confrontation between institutional
    authority and individual moral conviction, with the bridge serving
    as contested ground."

Every connection has a type, a strength, and a sentence saying what the connection actually consists of. The connections are extracted, scored, and traced back to the lines that support them; we don't editorialize on top. Where the scoring is uncertain, the strength score reflects that — connections below 0.7 surface for human review rather than landing in the canonical graph silently. The interesting question becomes what you want to ask.

§ 04 — one worked example

Mrs. Landingham dies at the end of Season 2. She does not stop changing the show.

Her relationship with Bartlet predates his political career. When she dies in “Two Cathedrals,” the next two seasons keep responding to that death — six subsequent episodes name her in dialogue, not as plot, but as the thing redirecting other characters' decisions.

A spreadsheet of characters loses all of this. The graph keeps it: her death is the source node for behavioural changes in other characters that propagate across S3 and into S4, each change carrying a pointer back to the line of script that justified it.

That's a query, not an essay. We can show you the cypher.

What that gets you: you can ask the show “which death is doing the most work right now?” in a writers' room — and answer it before the meeting ends.

§ 05 — engineering

It has to run unattended for days.

One season of TNG is sixty hours of LLM calls. If episode 14 fails halfway through, you don't want to start over. Fabula checkpoints after every episode and resumes from the last good state. Failed model calls retry, then fall back to a different model, then surface as a flagged extraction — they don't crash the run.

The log on the right is one such run: TNG Season 6, 26 episodes, 59.6 unattended hours, no episode-level failures. We make the same kind of report available for every season we process; ask and we'll send you others.
