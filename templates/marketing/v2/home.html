§ 01 — three verbs

Parse · Resolve · Reason

These are the three things Fabula does. Pick one to see the actual output.

Plain text, Fountain, PDF. No reformatting, no template. Fabula reads credits, scene headings, dialogue, action lines, and the smaller-than-a-scene unit that does the actual work — the beat.

A beat is one small movement inside a scene: someone enters, a request is made, a mood turns. Each one comes back with the characters involved, the objects in play, the setting, and the emotional shift it produces. That's the unit the rest of the graph hangs off.

The script calls Picard “The Captain” in one scene and “Jean-Luc” in the next. Worf gets promoted twice. Brigadier Lethbridge-Stewart returns across thirteen seasons of Doctor Who with new ranks, retirements, and multi-year gaps between appearances. Fabula keeps each of them as one entity, with the rank changes and absences recorded as transitions on that entity rather than as new ones.

When two entities really are different — two characters who happen to share a name, or two ships called Enterprise — Fabula makes the call explicitly and records why, with a confidence score. Adjudication, not vector similarity.

The Brigadier first appears in 1968 and last appears in 1989. Across that span — different uniforms, different writers, different Doctors beside him — the megagraph holds him as one canonical person.

For every event, Fabula records what each participating character believed, wanted, and intended at that moment — not as overall character traits, but as a per-scene state that changes when the show changes it. That's what lets you ask why a decision was made, not just what happened.

Every belief, goal, and connection comes back with a pointer to the specific lines of script that justified it. If you don't trust the claim, click through and read the source.

§ 02 — events have reasons

Most extraction stops at what happened.

Fabula keeps going. For every event in the show it records the connections to other events — typed, not just inferred from proximity. A connection is causal, or it carries a character forward, or it parallels a different event thematically, or it reverses one. Each connection is its own claim, with its own justification.

That's the difference between a transcript and a graph. A transcript can tell you Q showed up and Picard demanded his identity. The graph can tell you the second event happened because of the first, and that the same pattern — institutional authority confronted by individual conviction — recurs four other times across the season. The query for those four scenes is one line of Cypher, and every result carries the script lines that justify the connection. 

# Typed narrative connections · Star Trek TNG

  description: "Q's abrupt appearance and assertion of authority on the
    bridge leads directly to his ultimatum commanding humanity's
    retreat, provoking Picard's demand for Q's identity and Conn's
    readiness to fight."

- connection_type: CHARACTER_CONTINUITY
  strength: 0.88
  description: "Picard's immediate reaction to Conn being frozen
    — administering orders for medical aid and confronting Q —
    reflects his steadfast leadership and moral resolve."

- connection_type: THEMATIC_PARALLEL
  strength: 0.74
  description: "Both events stage a confrontation between institutional
    authority and individual moral conviction, with the bridge serving
    as contested ground."

Every connection has a type, a strength, and a sentence saying what the connection actually consists of. The connections are extracted, scored, and traced back to the lines that support them; we don't editorialize on top. Where the scoring is uncertain, the strength score reflects that — connections below 0.7 surface for human review rather than landing in the canonical graph silently. The interesting question becomes what you want to ask.

§ 04 — one worked example

Mrs. Landingham dies at the end of Season 2. She does not stop changing the show.

Her relationship with Bartlet predates his political career. When she dies in “Two Cathedrals,” the next two seasons keep responding to that death — six subsequent episodes name her in dialogue, not as plot, but as the thing redirecting other characters' decisions.

A spreadsheet of characters loses all of this. The graph keeps it: her death is the source node for behavioural changes in other characters that propagate across S3 and into S4, each change carrying a pointer back to the line of script that justified it.

That's a query, not an essay. We can show you the cypher.

§ 05 — engineering

It has to run unattended for days.

One season of TNG is sixty hours of LLM calls. If episode 14 fails halfway through, you don't want to start over. Fabula checkpoints after every episode and resumes from the last good state. Failed model calls retry, then fall back to a different model, then surface as a flagged extraction — they don't crash the run.

The log on the right is one such run: TNG Season 6, 26 episodes, 59.6 unattended hours, no episode-level failures. We make the same kind of report available for every season we process; ask and we'll send you others.

The graph is yours, in any format you read.
