§ 01 — three verbs

Parse · Resolve · Reason

These are the three things Fabula does. Pick one to see the actual output.

Plain text, Fountain, PDF. No reformatting, no template. Fabula reads credits, scene headings, dialogue, action lines, and the smaller-than-a-scene unit that does the actual work — the beat.

A beat is one small movement inside a scene: someone enters, a request is made, a mood turns. Each one comes back with the characters involved, the objects in play, the setting, and the emotional shift it produces. That's the unit the rest of the graph hangs off.

The script calls Picard “The Captain” in one scene and “Jean-Luc” in the next. Worf gets promoted twice. Brigadier Lethbridge-Stewart returns across thirteen seasons of Doctor Who with new ranks, retirements, and multi-year gaps between appearances. Fabula keeps each of them as one entity, with the rank changes and absences recorded as transitions on that entity rather than as new ones.

When two entities really are different — two characters who happen to share a name, or two ships called Enterprise — Fabula makes the call explicitly and records why, with a confidence score. Adjudication, not vector similarity.

The Brigadier first appears in 1968 and last appears in 1989. Across that span — different uniforms, different writers, different Doctors beside him — the megagraph holds him as one canonical person.

For every event, Fabula records what each participating character believed, wanted, and intended at that moment — per-scene state that changes when the show changes it. That's what lets you ask why a decision was made, not just what happened. The reasoning is not the model riffing from training data; it's the model adjudicating over an evidence packet built specifically for this step.

That packet is what we mean by context engineering: each pipeline step assembles a tight payload — the scene text, the entities already resolved, the prior state the character carried in — and forces the model's judgement onto what's in front of it. Every belief, goal, and connection in the output is grounded in those inputs and traceable back to the line of script that produced them.

§ 02 — events have reasons

Most extraction stops at what happened.

Fabula keeps going. For every event in the show it records the connections to other events — typed, not just inferred from proximity. A connection is causal, or it carries a character forward, or it parallels a different event thematically, or it reverses one. Each connection is its own claim, with its own justification.

A transcript tells you what happened; a graph tells you why—and shows patterns across the season, with each connection backed by the script.

Each connection has a type, strength, and a supporting sentence—automatically extracted and scored. Low-confidence links go to human review. The graph lets you ask deeper questions.

§ 05 — engineering

A self-correcting harness that can run unattended for days.

Fabula checkpoints after every episode and always resumes where it left off—no reprocessing needed. Failed model calls automatically retry, switch models, or flag the output, never crashing the run.

§ 06 — exports

The graph is yours, in any format you read.

Every published dataset ships as Parquet for analytics workflows, with a Cypher load script for Neo4j and the underlying graph in plain JSON for everything else. The directory below is the actual export of The West Wing Season 4. 

dataset_export/the_west_wing_s4/
├── nodes.parquet      # All graph nodes (id, label, name, description, properties)
├── edges.parquet      # All relationships (source, target, type, properties)
├── positions.parquet  # 3D layout coordinates with sizing and RGB colors
├── meta.json          # Dataset metadata: series info, entity counts
└── README.md          # HuggingFace dataset card (auto-generated)
