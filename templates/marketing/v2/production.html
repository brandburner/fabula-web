§ 01 — four ways shows lose the plot

Four failures every long-running show inherits.

failure 01

The silence in the room.

A continuity question lands in the writers' room. Someone is sure the answer is in Season 2. Someone else is sure it isn't. The room goes quiet while three people open three different documents.

The cost isn't the minute. It's the decisions that get made without waiting for the answer.

failure 02

The eighty-episode rewatch.

When the question is real and the stakes are real, someone has to actually go and watch. Or read. Or both. Eighty episodes of prior material, to confirm a single relationship state — a fact you already had on the page when the script was written, but no longer have on tap.

failure 03

The expensive contradiction.

The scene shoots. The contradiction surfaces in dailies. Or in the edit. Or, in the unfortunate case, in the cut after a critic notices. Reshoots, ADR, retroactive rewrites: the cost of a continuity problem caught after the money has been spent.

failure 04

The Friday afternoon read.

A new draft arrives at 4pm. Notes are due Monday. Nobody has time to re-read seventeen prior episodes to give them properly. So the notes go in based on memory, vibe, and whatever the assistant could pull together over the weekend.

§ 02 — alias resolution

Lieutenant Worf. Worf, Son of Mogh. Ambassador Worf. One person.

The script says “The Captain.” Later it says “Picard.” Later still, “Jean-Luc.” Your script coordinator knows these are the same person. Most extraction systems don't — they create three entries and call the problem solved.

Fabula resolves aliases against a living graph rather than re-running entity recognition for each script. Promotions don't create new characters. Klingon naming conventions don't create new characters. An ambassadorial appointment doesn't create a new character.

Across seven seasons and 177 episodes of TNG: cross-season identity preserved on Worf.

§ 03 — for ai builders

If you're building over story, here is your retrieval target.

Every result Fabula returns carries a confidence score and a pointer to the line of script that supports it. That's the bit most graph products skip, and it's what makes the data usable for any agent that has to cite its sources.

The hybrid graph-vector backend means you can ask “what episodes thematically parallel S2E14?” and “what is Picard's belief state in this scene?” with the same client. One does vector retrieval over scene embeddings; the other does a graph traversal over BDI states. The shape of the answer is the same.

Self-host the embeddings, self-host the graph, or use the API. Mix and match. 

$ curl https://api.fabula.productions/v1/query \
    -H "Authorization: Bearer $FABULA_KEY" \
    -d '{
      "series": "the_west_wing",
      "query": "scenes where Bartlet conceals the MS",
      "include": ["bdi", "provenance", "connections"]
    }'

  "avg_confidence": 0.93
}

§ 04 — what you get

In practice, on a real show.

Story bible, generated

The bible comes out of the graph. Every character, every relationship, every fact, every reference, with episode citations. It refreshes after every new script lands.

Continuity validation

New draft in. Conflicts surfaced before the table read: “this line contradicts S2E14, scene 4.” Caught Friday, fixed Monday.

Asset inventories

Every prop, location, costume, and reference, scoped by episode. Production design queries the graph instead of cross-referencing seventeen documents.

Character breakdowns

Casting briefs come out of the graph. Every speaking line, every beat, every relationship turn, with full episode and scene citations.

Precedent search

“Have we resolved a similar arc before?” Searchable across every episode the graph has ingested, including older shows you onboard later.

Audit trail

Every fact in the graph traces back to the line of script that produced it. The same provenance serves the writers' room, standards & practices, and legal.
