Stories Are Not Lines. They Are Webs.

For millennia, we've told stories as sequences—one thing after another. But we've always understood them as webs. A character's decision in Act One reverberates through Act Five. A location carries the weight of everything that happened there. Every story is a knowledge graph. We just haven't had the tools to see it until now.

{# ============================================ #} {# THE NARRATOLOGICAL FOUNDATION #} {# ============================================ #}

Fabula and Syuzhet: The Oldest Problem in Narrative Theory

In 1925, the Russian formalists articulated a distinction that has haunted narrative theory ever since. They called it fabula and syuzhet. Fabula is what happened—the chronological sequence of events in the story world. Syuzhet is how it's told—the order in which the audience encounters those events. Every flashback, every cold open, every non-linear narrative exploits the gap between the two.

This isn't an academic curiosity. It's a computational problem. To model a story properly, you need dual parallel linked lists with typed edges connecting them. When a writer introduces a flashback in Episode 15 that recontextualizes a scene from Episode 3, the knowledge graph needs to represent both the story-world sequence and the narrative presentation order—and the causal relationship between them. Most tools don't even try.

Why does this matter for production? Because the questions showrunners actually ask—“What did the audience know at this point in the story?” and “What had actually happened by this point in the timeline?”—are fundamentally different queries, and answering them correctly requires maintaining both indices. Flashback impact analysis. Causal chain traversal. Audience knowledge state at any point in the narrative. These are graph operations, and they require graph infrastructure.

We didn't invent this framework. Gérard Genette described it in 1972. We're the first to implement it as production-grade software.

{# ============================================ #} {# WHY GRAPHS, NOT DOCUMENTS #} {# ============================================ #}

Why a Knowledge Graph, Not a Better Spreadsheet

Spreadsheets can list. Wikis can describe. But only graphs can answer. The question “Which characters have scenes with both the President and the First Lady, and how did those relationships change after the MS scandal?” requires traversal—following typed edges across temporal boundaries, filtering by relationship state, aggregating across episodes. Try that in a spreadsheet. Try it in a wiki. You can't. It's a graph operation.

We use Neo4j for structural queries and relationship traversal—the “find all paths between these two characters” queries that graph databases were invented to answer. We use ChromaDB for semantic intelligence—the “find entities similar to this description” queries that require embedding space rather than explicit edges. The combination gives us both relational precision and semantic flexibility.

But the deepest architectural decision is subtler than the database choice. It's the separation of identity from state. A Character node represents who someone is—their canonical identity, their aliases, their persistent attributes. An EventParticipation represents who they are in a specific moment—their emotional state, their goals, what they did. This separation is what allows a knowledge graph to answer “how did this character change over time?” without overwriting their history. It's the difference between a database and a document. Documents flatten. Graphs preserve.

What We Believe

“Stories deserve better tools than spreadsheets.”

The entertainment industry creates the most complex narrative structures in human culture. It manages them in Google Sheets.

Schema constraints, evidence grounding, confidence gating, contrastive resolution. We treat hallucination the way databases treat corruption: with layers of defence.

Other tools extract entities. We extract the relationships between them—causal, thematic, emotional, temporal—because narrative meaning lives in the edges, not just the nodes.

“IP is a graph, not a document.”

When your show's narrative structure is a queryable graph, every downstream use case—game adaptation, interactive media, AI-assisted writing—becomes a query, not a rewrite.
